Is AI ready to take over quantum theory?

No. Not in the sense the question implies.

There is no LLM that can independently identify a problem, develop physical intuition
for why it matters, choose the right formalism, execute the calculation reliably,
interpret the result physically, and write it up. That chain still requires a
physicist. Nothing I've shown you today changes that.

But I want to suggest that "taking over" is the wrong image — and that fixing on it
lets us avoid the more uncomfortable question.

---

### The shift already happening

Look at what Hsu actually did with his time.

He did not do the algebra. He designed the Generate-Verify protocol. He caught the
Reeh-Schlieder false direction — which required him to work through a theorem from
axiomatic QFT he hadn't previously needed. He evaluated the causal structure of the
KR model. He decided what to pursue and what to discard.

Look at what the physicists behind the Hartree-Fock study actually did. They did not
execute the derivation steps. They designed the template. They supplied the implicit
notation — the things every condensed matter physicist knows and no paper writes down.
They corrected intermediate steps. GPT-4 scored above 95 on mathematical rigor. They
decided what that meant.

The physicist's role is already shifting. Not away from physics — upward within it.
Toward judgment, scaffolding, and the things that require knowing not just how to
calculate but which calculation is worth making.

That is not a smaller role. But it is a different one.

### What mathematics is showing us

[Slide from §5 if still up, or a single line: "Software → Mathematics → Physics"]

You've already heard the Erdős case in detail. Let me just say what it means from here.

Proof lengths are scaling toward the length of a publishable physics paper. Human
referees are stretched thin. A Fields Medal is predicted by 2030. And Tao's phrase —
proof indigestion — describes not a future risk but a present operational problem:
more verified results than the community can absorb and understand.

Mathematics is not theoretical physics. But it is close enough in formalism and rigor
to function as a leading indicator — and the distance between mathematics and physics
on this adoption curve is shorter than the distance the curve has already covered.

The physicist who dismisses this because physics has experimental constraints and
requires physical intuition is making the same argument that was made about chess,
about formal proof, about code. In each case, the capability arrived earlier than
the argument predicted.

---

### The question it leaves open

In 1926, the Schrödinger equation was written. The question among physicists was not
whether to use it. It was what it meant. The probabilistic interpretation took years
to settle. The Born rule was contested. The equation did not wait for the argument
to conclude.

In 2026, results are being written partly by machines. The question among physicists
is not whether to use the tools — that question is already being answered, quietly,
in labs and offices where LLMs are used and not disclosed. The question is what it
means. What it means for how we train physicists. What it means for what we value in
a result. What it means for the transmission of tacit knowledge — the things that live
in the hands of experienced researchers and don't survive being converted into
template prompts.

Max Perutz, who founded the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology and won the Nobel
Prize for the structure of hemoglobin, asked in 1989: "Does humanity need science?"
His answer was yes. But Perutz also opened that essay with a false dialectic —
one the *Nature* editorial quotes directly:

*"Is scientific research the noblest pursuit of the human mind, from which springs
a never-ceasing stream of beneficial discoveries, or is it a sorcerer's broom
that threatens us all with destruction?"*

The editorial's response: *"These opposing extremes, both true in their own way,
should not be allowed to distort AI's true potential — or obscure its limitations."*

This is exactly the binary this talk has been trying to hold open. "Taking over"
is the sorcerer's broom version. "Just autocomplete" is the noble-pursuit version
that dismisses what the evidence actually shows. Both are wrong. The evidence
is more interesting than either.

*Nature*'s editorial board, in the same issue, inverted Perutz's original question
for 2026: Does science need humanity?

Their answer is also yes. So is mine. But the grounds keep shifting — and the case
that needs to be made keeps having to be remade on new terrain.
