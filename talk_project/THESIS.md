## Current Status: THESIS SETTLED (working version — subject to revision after §1 and §6 drafts)

**Working thesis sentence**:
> *LLMs have crossed from tools into contributors at the calculation and conjecture levels
> of theoretical physics — still requiring expert oversight, but already appearing in
> published results — and mathematics is showing us, in real time, what happens when
> that oversight requirement starts to erode.*

**Rhetorical structure**: Empirical claim (present) → honest qualification → prediction
grounded in evidence (not speculation). This survives being said aloud and cross-examined.

**Closing line** (§6, not the thesis itself):
> *The expert bottleneck is the last line of defense between current LLMs and
> autonomous physics research — and mathematics is showing us, in real time,
> what happens when it starts to give way.*

**What settled it**: The three-paper structure (Hsu / Pan et al. / Lu et al.) plus the
Castelvecchi mathematics evidence made it possible to claim *demonstrated present
contribution* rather than *future potential*, while the expert bottleneck framing gives
the qualification teeth. Candidate A (tool/reasoner) was retired because the execution/
judgment reframe (§4) is more durable; Candidate B (inevitability) became the frame
rather than the thesis; Candidate C (epistemological) feeds into §2.9 and §5.6 but is
too broad to anchor a 45-minute talk.

## The Central Question the Title Poses

"Is AI ready to take over quantum theory?"

This is a deliberately provocative framing. The honest answer is almost certainly "no,
not in the sense the question implies" — but the interesting work is in specifying
*why not*, *what it can do*, and *what the trajectory suggests*.

The talk should not dodge the question. It should answer it — with precision.

## Candidate Thesis Statements

### Candidate A: The Tool/Reasoner Distinction
> *LLMs are becoming genuinely powerful tools for theoretical physics — for literature
> synthesis, conjecture generation, and cross-domain pattern recognition — but remain
> fundamentally limited as reasoners. Physics is uniquely positioned both to exploit
> LLM strengths and to expose LLM limitations. Understanding this distinction is what
> separates productive adoption from either hype or dismissal.*

**Strengths:**
- Empirically defensible; Hsu case study fits this frame well
- Gives the audience a usable conceptual tool, not just a verdict
- Honest about limitations without being dismissive
- Avoids the hype/fear binary

**Weaknesses / Counterarguments:**
- "Tool vs. reasoner" distinction may not survive contact with o1-class models and
  beyond — reasoning capabilities are improving rapidly
- Risks feeling like a hedge rather than an argument
- May already be somewhat dated by the time of the talk

**Open question:** Is the tool/reasoner distinction stable, or is it a temporary
description of a moving frontier?

---

### Candidate B: The Inevitability Thesis
> *LLM integration into theoretical physics research is inevitable — the trajectory
> from software engineering makes this clear. The question is not whether but how.
> Physicists who understand this now will shape how it happens; those who resist will
> simply be shaped by it.*

**Strengths:**
- Gives the talk a clear action-oriented message
- Speaker's authority here is genuine — lived experience in software
- Has emotional resonance for an audience at the fear-and-loathing stage
- True, as far as it goes

**Weaknesses / Counterarguments:**
- Physics is genuinely different from software in ways that matter:
  formal rigor, mathematical proof, the role of physical intuition
- "Inevitable" can sound dismissive of legitimate concerns
- Risks being a motivational talk rather than a scientific one
- Needs to be grounded in evidence, not just analogy

**Open question:** What is the strongest case that physics is *different enough* from
software that the analogy breaks down? The talk should steelman this.

---

### Candidate C: The Epistemological Thesis
> *The Hsu paper represents something genuinely new in the epistemology of physics:
> a published result whose originating conjecture was not generated by a human mind.
> This is not a crisis — it is a shift in what counts as a source of physical intuition.
> Understanding it clearly is more useful than either celebrating or deploring it.*

**Strengths:**
- Most intellectually ambitious of the three candidates
- Connects naturally to the audience's likely interests (foundations, measurement theory)
- The Hsu case study carries more weight under this framing
- Has genuine novelty as an argument

**Weaknesses / Counterarguments:**
- Harder to execute well — risks becoming vague or philosophical
- Requires careful treatment of what "conjecture generation" actually means
  (is a pattern-matched suggestion from a language model really a conjecture?)
- May be more than a 45-minute talk can sustain

**Open question:** Can this be made precise enough to be defensible in a physics talk,
or does it require more philosophical apparatus than the format allows?

## Tensions to Resolve

1. **The moving-target problem**: LLM capabilities are advancing during the preparation
   of this talk. Whatever is said about current limitations may be partially obsolete
   by delivery. How to frame claims to be robust to this?

2. **The private-use phenomenon**: If researchers are already using LLMs without
   disclosing it, the published literature systematically underrepresents actual adoption.
   What can be said responsibly about this?

3. **The hallucination problem in context**: LLMs hallucinate citations and results.
   This is especially dangerous in physics, where a false result can propagate. Does
   this argue for the tool/reasoner distinction? Or is it a solvable engineering problem?

4. **The Hsu question**: Is the Hsu paper a proof of concept, an outlier, or the
   beginning of a trend? The evidence needed to distinguish these is not yet in.

---

## Evidence the Thesis Must Account For

- **For optimism**: Hsu paper (published, novel, LLM-originated conjecture);
  AI Feynman / symbolic regression results; benchmark performance on graduate physics
- **For skepticism**: Hallucination rates on technical content; failure modes on
  formal proof; the gap between exam performance and research-level contribution
- **For complexity**: The "private use" phenomenon; the social media / preprint layer;
  the speed of capability improvement making any snapshot claim unstable

## Working Hypothesis (Not Yet a Thesis)

The talk's most defensible and interesting position is probably a synthesis of
Candidates A and C, anchored by the inevitability frame of Candidate B:

> LLMs have crossed a threshold — evidenced by the Hsu paper and a growing body of
> practice — where they can function as genuine contributors to the conjecture phase
> of theoretical physics research. They remain unreliable reasoners. This is not a
> stable equilibrium: capability is improving. Physicists should understand this
> distinction now, because how the field responds in the next few years will shape
> what physics looks like in the next few decades. The analogy to software engineering
> is imperfect but instructive: the transition happened faster than predicted, with
> less disruption than feared, and more transformation than imagined.
